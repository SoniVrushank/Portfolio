SEO Keywords, Explained: How to Find and Use Them

A beginner's guide, written the way I'd explain it to a client.

SEO keywords are the words and phrases people actually type into Google when they're looking for something you offer. Keyword research is the process of finding those exact phrases, then using them — naturally — in your titles, headings, and page copy, so search engines understand what the page is about and match it to the right searches. Done well, it's less about tricking an algorithm and more about describing your page the way your customer already thinks about it.

What actually counts as a keyword

A keyword isn't always a single word. "Shoes" is a keyword. So is "best running shoes for flat feet under ₹5000." Both are valid — they just serve different jobs, at different points in someone's search.

Short-tail vs long-tail keywords

Short-tail keywords are broad — one or two words, high search volume, and brutally competitive ("marketing agency," "jewellery online"). Long-tail keywords are longer and more specific ("digital marketing agency for jewellery brands in Ahmedabad"). They get searched less often, but the person searching knows exactly what they want — which usually means they're closer to buying, and you're competing against far fewer pages to reach them.

Search intent is the part most people skip

Before targeting a keyword, ask what the searcher actually wants:

  • Informational — "what is SEO" (they want to learn)
  • Commercial — "best SEO tools 2026" (they're comparing options)
  • Transactional — "hire SEO freelancer" (they're ready to act)
  • Navigational — "Google Search Console login" (they want a specific site)

Writing a product page for an informational keyword — or a blog post for a transactional one — is one of the most common reasons content doesn't rank, even when the keyword itself was a good pick.

Where to find keywords, for free

  • Start typing your topic into Google and read the autosuggest list — those are real, common phrases
  • Scroll to "People also ask" and "Related searches" at the bottom of the results page
  • Check Google Search Console on a site you already run — it shows the exact queries people used to find you
  • Read a competitor's page and note the phrases they repeat in headings

You don't need a paid tool to start. You need ten minutes on Google and a notes app.

Where to place them once you have them

Keyword placement matters more than keyword quantity. Put your primary keyword in the title tag, the H1, and naturally within the first 100 words. Use it once or twice more across the page's subheadings, in the URL if possible, and in an image's alt text where it genuinely describes the image. Everywhere else, write for the person reading — not the crawler.

The mistake that undoes all of it

Keyword stuffing — repeating a phrase unnaturally, over and over — reads badly to people and gets discounted by search engines anyway. The fix is simple: write the sentence you'd actually say out loud, then check whether the keyword fits in naturally. If it doesn't, use a close variation instead of forcing it.

FAQ

What's the difference between SEO and keywords?

SEO is the overall practice of making a site easier to find in search. Keywords are the specific terms you optimize around inside that practice — one part of a bigger process.

How many keywords should one page target?

One primary keyword plus a small handful of closely related terms. Spreading a single page across many unrelated keywords usually weakens its ranking for all of them.

Do I need a paid tool to do keyword research?

No. Google's own autosuggest, the "People also ask" box, related searches, and Search Console cover most of what a small business needs to get started.

How long does SEO take to show results?

Usually a few months for new content to get indexed, evaluated, and start climbing. It's a slow, compounding channel rather than an instant one.
